Theoretical Background

The π-Stack Optimizer solves the problem of finding energetically favorable configurations for molecular stacks. This is achieved by combining quantum chemical energy evaluations with global optimization algorithms.

Parameter Space Definition

The geometry of the molecular stack is defined by a set of parameters that describe the rigid body transformation of the monomer and its internal conformational flexibility. The total parameter space has dimensions \(7 + N\), where \(N\) is the number of flexible dihedral angles.

Rigid Body Parameters (7 Dimensions)

The relative position and orientation of the monomer unit in the stack are described by:

  1. Rotation Angle (:math:`theta`): To avoid discontinuities and periodic boundary issues associated with angles (e.g., \(0 \approx 2\pi\)), the rotation is encoded using its sine and cosine components:

    \[c_\theta = \cos(\theta), \quad s_\theta = \sin(\theta)\]

    The optimization algorithms evolve \(c_\theta\) and \(s_\theta\) independently, and the actual angle is reconstructed as \(\theta = \text{atan2}(s_\theta, c_\theta)\).

  2. Translation Vector (:math:`mathbf{T}`): Three parameters describe the translation of the monomer along the Cartesian axes:

    \[\mathbf{T} = [T_x, T_y, T_z]\]

  3. Rotation Center (:math:`mathbf{C}`): Two parameters define the effective center of rotation in the X-Y plane (perpendicular to the stacking axis, usually Z):

    \[\mathbf{C} = [C_x, C_y]\]

    Combined with a fixed \(C_z = 0\), this allows for off-axis stacking arrangements.

Flexible Parameters (\(N\) Dimensions)

For molecules with internal flexibility, specific rotatable bonds can be defined. Each bond \(i\) contributes one degree of freedom:

\[\boldsymbol{\tau} = [\tau_1, \tau_2, \dots, \tau_N]\]

These are dihedral angles that modify the monomer’s internal structure before the rigid body transformation is applied.

Objective Function

The goal of the optimization is to minimize the binding energy per monomer in the stack.

Energy Calculation

The binding energy (\(E_{bind}\)) is calculated using the Semi-Empirical Tight-Binding (xTB) method (specifically GFN2-xTB by default). For a system of \(N\) monomers (typically a dimer, \(N=2\)) with total energy \(E_{stack}\) and isolated monomer energy \(E_{mono}\):

\[E_{bind} = \frac{E_{stack} - N \cdot E_{mono}}{N - 1}\]

Penalty Functions

To ensure physically realistic and computationally viable configurations, several penalty terms are added to the energy:

  1. Hard Clash Penalty: If any two atoms between adjacent monomers are closer than a cutoff distance (\(d_{inter} < 1.6 \text{ \AA}\)):

    \[P_{clash} = 1000.0\]

  2. Intramolecular Clash Penalty: For flexible molecules, if atoms within the monomer overlap (\(d_{intra} < 1.2 \text{ \AA}\)):

    \[P_{intra} = 1000.0\]

  3. SCF Convergence Failure: If the xTB calculation fails to converge (e.g., due to extreme geometry distortion):

    \[P_{fail} = 1,000,000\]

The final objective function value is:

\[f(\mathbf{x}) = E_{bind} + P_{clash} + P_{intra} + P_{fail}\]
